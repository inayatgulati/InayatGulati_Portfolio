Overview

The Pavilion

The Sculpture Pavilion is a conceptual architectural design serving as an interactive exhibition space for sculptures. The project focuses on layered panelling, spatial hierarchy, and dynamic light interactions — offering visitors a unique sensory experience as they navigate the space.

Rhino 3D Adobe Illustrator Adobe Photoshop Spatial Design Layered Architecture
1
Project Goals

Design Intentions

Four principles guided every decision — from panel placement to visitor pathways.

Immersive multi-level exhibition space
Create a space where sculpture displays feel embedded in the architecture itself — not just placed within a neutral container.
Integrate light and shadow dynamics
Use strategic panel angles and voids to generate a rhythmic interplay of light and shadow that shifts throughout the day, enhancing spatial engagement.
Design clear navigation pathways
Ensure fluid visitor movement through the space — dual staircases offer two distinct journeys through the same architectural experience.
Apply layered architecture principles
Balance aesthetics with functionality through overlapping, intersecting panels that create depth and rhythm without sacrificing structural clarity.
2
Challenges & Solutions

Three Problems,
Three Answers

Each structural and experiential challenge demanded a deliberate architectural response.

Challenge 01
Fluid navigation through open space
An open exhibition space risks disorienting visitors — no clear sense of direction or flow.
Solution: Defined pathways using layered panels and clear entry/exit points that guide movement without enclosing it.
Challenge 02
Structural integrity vs. openness
An open, airy design risks structural instability — especially across a two-story span.
Solution: Staggered and intersecting panels maintain form while enhancing visual openness — structure becomes the aesthetic.
Challenge 03
Maximising light without overwhelming
Too much direct light flattens the sculpture; too little creates a dim, uninviting atmosphere.
Solution: Voids placed in key panel areas allow controlled natural light — dappled, directional, and purposeful.

Design Process

From Sketch
to Structure

Four iterative phases took the pavilion from a rough two-story concept to a spatially nuanced, light-driven architectural experience.

Step 01 — Initial Sketch & Conceptualisation
Two-story structure, inclined roof

The initial design envisioned a two-story structure with an inclined roof, emphasising symmetry and balance. Early explorations in Rhino revealed the potential for enhancing spatial generosity and refining movement pathways beyond the original sketch.

Initial sketch — two-story concept with inclined roof and symmetrical layout
Step 02 — Refining Structure & Layout
Rules 1 & 3 of layered architecture

Panels were designed to overlap and intersect, creating depth and rhythm — with open voids for natural light penetration and a structured yet flexible navigation system.

Refined structure — overlapping panels, void placement, layered depth
Step 03 — Circulation & Interaction Design
Dual staircases, two journeys

Visitors can ascend from the right, following a structured route through sculptures — or start at the upper level, enjoying a bird's-eye view before descending. Same pavilion, two completely different experiences.

Circulation diagram — dual staircase pathways and visitor flow routes
Step 04 — Light & Shadow Experimentation
Panels as light instruments

By positioning panels at strategic angles, the design creates a rhythmic interplay of light and shadow that dynamically shifts throughout the day — enhancing the sculptures' visual prominence while providing a changing visitor experience.

Final Outcome

What Was Built

Three outcomes that define the pavilion's success as both an architectural and experiential project.

A visually engaging pavilion
Seamlessly blends art with architecture — the structure itself becomes part of the exhibition, not just a backdrop to it.
A multi-pathway exploration experience
Visitors engage with sculptures from different perspectives depending on which route they take — the same works feel different from above versus below.
A rhythmic, light-sensitive environment
Movement through the space changes how art is perceived — light shifts, shadows move, and the pavilion becomes a living frame for the sculptures within it.
6
Key Takeaways

What I Learned

Three architectural insights that will carry into every spatial design project that follows.

Spatial Hierarchy and Layered Architecture

Enhance both function and form through thoughtful organisation of space and materials. The best architectural decisions serve both — never one at the expense of the other.

Interactivity Through Circulation

Perspective shifts improve visitor engagement and create dynamic experiences. A space that offers multiple paths is a space that rewards multiple visits.

Light as an Architectural Element

Can elevate a space from static to dynamic, transforming how visitors perceive art. Light isn't decoration — in this pavilion, it's structure.

The biggest realisation was that architecture is choreography. Every panel, void, and staircase is an instruction to the visitor's body — telling it where to look, pause, and move next.
