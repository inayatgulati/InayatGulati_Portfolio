Overview

The Project

Sojoy Scribbles is a fully responsive, multi-page e-commerce site that reflects a distinct brand personality — bright, expressive, and user-friendly — while meeting accessibility and interaction standards.

5
Full pages
Home, Products, Cart, Billing, and Order Confirmation — a complete end-to-end shopping flow
Brand-first

Soft pastel palette, illustrated icons, and friendly typography echoing the handcrafted nature of the stickers
Accessible
ARIA standards
Buttons, labels, and form fields optimised with ARIA attributes and mobile-friendly responsive layouts

"Crafting joy, one scribble at a time — each page should feel personal and handcrafted, echoing the unique nature of the stickers themselves."

Design Concept

Warm, Expressive
& Personal

The visual system was designed to feel like the stickers themselves — playful, personal, and immediately recognisable as something made with care.

🎨
Soft pastel palette and friendly typography
Warmth and approachability — every colour choice reinforces the handcrafted, joyful nature of the brand without feeling childish.
🖼
Illustrated icons and imagery
A cohesive art-centric identity where the sticker artwork is always the hero — the UI frames and supports it rather than competing with it.
⬛
Grid-based layouts
Consistent balance and readability across all five pages — especially important on the product grid where visual density could easily become overwhelming.

I contributed to the layout design, page structuring, and interaction styling across all major pages — ensuring consistency between the homepage, product listing, and checkout experience.

Reflection

What I Learned

Building a full e-commerce site from design to shipped code deepened my understanding of design systems, user flow consistency, and front-end accessibility.

Web design systems
Maintaining visual consistency across five different pages required a shared component approach — colour, spacing, and typography had to be systematised from the start, not retrofitted at the end.
User flow consistency
Every design decision on the cart page affected what users expected on the billing page — thinking in flows rather than individual screens made the checkout experience feel seamless.
Accessibility in practice
Implementing ARIA standards wasn't just a checkbox — it forced better form design, clearer error states, and more intentional button labelling that improved the experience for everyone.
Design into code
Translating visual designs into working HTML and CSS revealed edge cases the Figma prototype couldn't surface — responsive behaviour, hover states, and form validation all required iteration in the browser.
Future Iterations
🔍
Search and filter system
A responsive search bar and product filters for better discovery — especially important as the sticker catalogue grows.
💾
Cart persistence via local storage
Cart state should survive page refreshes — users lose confidence in a store when their basket empties on reload.
🌙
Light / Dark mode toggle
Inclusive design that respects system preferences — especially relevant for a pastel-heavy brand where dark mode would require a considered palette shift.
